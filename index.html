Q01

Walk me through how you'd design a batch data pipeline end-to-end.

system-designarchitectureopener
How to think about this

This is the classic opener and it's really a test of structure, not trivia. The interviewer wants to see that you can decompose a fuzzy ask into clean layers, and that you reach for requirements before technology. The single biggest signal: do you ask clarifying questions first? Volume, latency SLA, source systems, who consumes the output, and how fresh it must be — these change the whole design.

Answer in layers that map to the data's journey: ingest → land → transform → serve, with orchestration, quality, and observability as cross-cutting concerns. Narrate the data flowing through, not a bag of AWS services.

Decision justification

I separate storage from compute (land raw in S3, transform with Glue/Spark) so I can reprocess history without re-pulling from sources, scale compute independently, and keep an immutable audit trail. I land raw data unmodified first (the "extract" is cheap and the source may not be queryable later), then transform in stages so a bug in business logic never forces a re-extract. I make every stage idempotent and partitioned by run date so retries and backfills are safe.

Full answer

"Before I draw anything, I'd nail down: what are the sources and their formats? What's the data volume and growth? What freshness does the business need — hourly, daily? Who consumes the output and through what interface? Let me assume a daily batch from a few operational databases and event files, feeding a warehouse for analytics."

Then the layered design:

  • Ingestion / extract. Pull from sources on a schedule. RDBMS via JDBC or CDC; files via S3 events; SaaS via APIs. Land bytes as-is into a raw zone, partitioned by source/table/dt=YYYY-MM-DD.
  • Raw (bronze) zone. Immutable, append-only, schema-on-read. This is your replay buffer — if downstream logic changes, you reprocess from here.
  • Transform / curate (silver). Cleanse, dedup, conform types, apply business rules with Spark/Glue. Write columnar (Parquet) partitioned for query patterns.
  • Serve (gold). Aggregate into facts/dimensions or marts loaded into Redshift/Snowflake, or exposed via the Glue Catalog + Athena.
  • Orchestration. Airflow/MWAA or Step Functions sequences the stages, handles retries, and enforces dependencies.
  • Cross-cutting: data-quality checks between stages (gate before serving), a metadata/catalog layer for discovery, and observability (freshness, row counts, latency, failures).

I'd close by stating the SLA path: "raw lands by 02:00, transforms finish by 04:00, marts refreshed by 05:00, with alerting if any stage misses its window."

⚑ Remember this

"Land raw first, transform later" is the move that wins points. It means every business-logic bug is a reprocess, not a re-extract — and reprocessing is cheap when storage and compute are separate.

What not to say

Don't jump straight to "I'd use Glue and Redshift" without asking about volume, latency, or consumers — naming services before requirements is the #1 junior tell.

Don't describe a single monolithic job that extracts, transforms, and loads in one step. That couples your business logic to your source and makes backfills a nightmare.

Q02

Batch vs streaming vs micro-batch — how do you choose?

architecturestreamingtrade-offs
How to think about this

The interviewer wants to know you won't reach for streaming because it's exciting. The deciding question is always "what's the latency the business actually needs, and what does it cost to deliver it?" Streaming is strictly more operationally expensive than batch — more moving parts, harder reprocessing, harder testing. So the burden of proof is on streaming.

Anchor your answer on the freshness requirement and the cost of staleness. Fraud detection where a 5-minute delay means money lost? Streaming. A daily revenue dashboard the CFO reads at 9am? Batch, every time.

Decision justification

I default to batch because it's cheap, trivially reprocessable (just re-run the job), easy to test, and easy to reason about. I move to micro-batch (Spark Structured Streaming, trigger every N seconds/minutes) when I need minutes-fresh data but want to keep batch-like tooling and exactly-once via checkpointing. I only go true streaming (Flink, sub-second, per-event) when sub-minute latency genuinely changes the outcome — and I accept the complexity tax: state management, watermarks, out-of-order handling, and a separate reprocessing story.

Full answer

"It comes down to latency requirement weighed against operational cost. I'd ask: how fresh does this need to be, and what breaks if it's an hour stale?"

  • Batch — bounded data processed on a schedule. Best for the vast majority of analytics: reporting, ML training sets, daily aggregations. Cheapest, most testable, reprocessing is just re-running.
  • Micro-batch — Spark Structured Streaming processes tiny batches every few seconds/minutes. A pragmatic middle ground: near-real-time freshness with checkpoint-based exactly-once and familiar Spark code. Great for dashboards that need to be minutes-fresh.
  • True streaming — Flink/Kafka Streams, event-at-a-time, sub-second. Reserved for fraud, real-time personalization, alerting, ops monitoring — where every second of staleness has a cost.

I'd mention the Lambda vs Kappa debate: Lambda runs a batch and a streaming path in parallel (accurate + fast, but two codebases to maintain); Kappa treats everything as a stream and reprocesses by replaying the log (one codebase, simpler, now usually preferred). And I'd note that you can often get "real-time enough" by simply running a batch more frequently before you reach for streaming infrastructure.

Q03

What makes a pipeline idempotent, and how do you get exactly-once?

idempotencycorrectnessmust-know
How to think about this

This is a non-negotiable senior concept. Idempotency means running the same job twice produces the same result as running it once — no duplicates, no double-counting. The interviewer is checking whether you design for a world where everything retries: tasks fail mid-write, schedulers fire twice, someone reruns yesterday. If your pipeline isn't idempotent, retries corrupt data, and you can't safely backfill.

Frame "exactly-once" honestly: end-to-end exactly-once delivery is famously hard. What you actually engineer is at-least-once delivery + idempotent writes = effectively-once processing. That nuance is the senior answer.

Decision justification

I make writes idempotent rather than chase true exactly-once delivery because it's simpler and more robust. The two workhorse patterns: (1) partition overwrite — a job that processes dt=2026-06-08 deletes-and-rewrites that whole partition, so rerunning replaces rather than appends; (2) MERGE/upsert on a natural key — into a warehouse or a transactional lake table (Iceberg/Delta/Hudi), so re-processed rows update in place. I prefer overwrite for immutable daily snapshots and MERGE when rows mutate or arrive late.

Full answer

"Idempotent means re-running yields the same state. I assume tasks retry and schedulers occasionally double-fire, so I never blindly append."

Concretely:

  • Deterministic partitioning + overwrite. Each run owns a partition keyed by logical date. Writing uses overwrite of that partition only (Spark partitionOverwriteMode=dynamic). Rerun = same partition rewritten, no dupes.
  • MERGE on a business key. For warehouses/lakehouse tables, upsert keyed on the natural key so a row processed twice updates instead of duplicating.
  • Atomic publish. Write to a temp/staging location, then atomically swap (rename, or table-format commit). Readers never see half-written output.
  • Dedup with a stable key. Carry a deterministic event/record id; dedup on it so at-least-once sources don't inflate counts.

"For exactly-once I'm honest: I get at-least-once from the source plus idempotent writes, which is effectively-once. In Spark Structured Streaming, checkpoints + idempotent sinks give exactly-once within the engine; across system boundaries I rely on the dedup key."

⚑ Gotcha

Plain df.write.mode("append") is the classic idempotency bug. One retry after a partial failure and you've silently double-counted revenue. Overwrite a partition or MERGE — never blind-append in a job that can retry.

What not to say

Don't claim you "guarantee exactly-once" end-to-end without qualification — anyone senior will probe it and you'll fold. Say "at-least-once + idempotent writes = effectively-once."

Don't propose "check if the row exists before inserting" as your scaling answer — that's a race condition and a per-row round-trip. Use set-based MERGE or partition overwrite.

Q04

How do you design for backfills and reprocessing without double-counting?

backfillidempotencyoperations
How to think about this

Backfill is where junior pipelines die. The interviewer wants evidence you treat "reprocess any historical window safely" as a first-class requirement, not an afterthought. The whole answer flows from one idea: a job must be a pure function of its input partition and logical date — never of "now," never of mutable external state.

Decision justification

I parameterize every job by a logical execution date rather than wall-clock today(), because backfilling June 1st must produce exactly what June 1st produced originally. I combine that with idempotent partition overwrite (from Q3) so re-running a date range replaces those partitions cleanly. I avoid designs that read "the latest state of a dimension" non-deterministically, because that makes old reruns produce new (wrong) answers.

Full answer

"Two requirements make backfills safe: determinism and idempotency."

  • Parameterize by logical date. The job takes ds (the date being processed) as input and reads only that partition. Never call current_date() inside transform logic — that bakes in non-determinism.
  • Idempotent writes. Each date overwrites its own partition, so a backfill of 90 days just loops the job over 90 dates, each replacing its slice.
  • Bounded, parallel-safe backfills. Because partitions are independent, I can run dates in parallel — but I cap concurrency to protect source systems and the warehouse.
  • Watch the dimension-drift trap. If a fact joins to a dimension's "current" value, reprocessing old data picks up today's dimension — wrong. Use point-in-time / SCD-2 joins (the value as of that date) when history must be faithful.
  • Track lineage/audit. Stamp each row/partition with the run id and processing timestamp so you can prove what was reprocessed and when.

"In Airflow this maps directly: catchup=True with {{ ds }} templating runs one idempotent task instance per missing interval — that is the backfill mechanism."

Joining today's facts to a dimension's current attributes seems fine — until a backfill rewrites two-year-old facts with the customer's new address/segment. If history must be faithful, join on the dimension value as of that date (SCD-2 / point-in-time).

What not to say

Don't say "I'd just truncate and reload everything." That's expensive, risky, and impossible at scale — and it hammers source systems.

Don't ignore the dimension-drift problem; mentioning point-in-time joins is a strong senior signal.

Q05

How do you design a partitioning strategy for a data lake?

data-lakeS3performance
How to think about this

Partitioning is the single biggest lever on lake query cost and performance, so the interviewer wants to see you choose partitions from how the data is queried — not arbitrarily. The mental model: a partition column lets the engine skip reading whole folders (partition pruning). Partition on what appears in WHERE filters; don't partition on high-cardinality columns or you create the small-files problem.

Decision justification

I almost always partition by date (dt=YYYY-MM-DD) because nearly every analytical query and every incremental load is time-bounded, and it aligns partitions with how data arrives and is reprocessed. I add a second low-cardinality dimension (region, event_type) only if queries consistently filter on it. I deliberately avoid partitioning on high-cardinality keys (user_id, order_id) because that explodes into millions of tiny files, wrecking both S3 listing and Spark task scheduling. For high-cardinality access I reach for bucketing or Z-ordering/clustering instead.

Full answer

"Partitioning is physical layout that enables pruning — the engine reads only the folders matching the filter. So I design partitions around query and load patterns."

  • Primary: time. dt=2026-06-08. Matches incremental loads, retention/expiry, and the time filters in most queries.
  • Secondary: a low-cardinality, frequently-filtered column. e.g. region=us. Keep total partitions reasonable — aim for partitions in the 100s of MB to ~1GB each.
  • Cardinality discipline. Each partition should hold enough data to produce healthy file sizes (128MB–1GB). Too granular ⇒ millions of tiny files ⇒ slow listing and scheduling overhead.
  • File format + within-file pruning. Store Parquet/ORC; their column stats + min/max enable predicate pushdown within a partition, so partitioning and columnar formats compound.
  • Catalog scaling. With huge partition counts, prefer partition projection (Athena/Glue computes partitions from a pattern) over crawler-registered partitions to avoid metastore bloat.

Don't suggest partitioning by a high-cardinality id "for fast lookups" — that's the small-files anti-pattern; use bucketing/clustering.

Don't forget the catalog cost — millions of registered partitions slow down query planning; mention partition projection.

Q06

How do you handle late-arriving and out-of-order data?

event-timewatermarkscorrectness
How to think about this

The key insight the interviewer is fishing for: event time ≠ processing time. An event that happened at 23:58 might arrive at 00:05 the next day. If you bucket by arrival time you misattribute it. You need a deliberate policy for "how late is too late," and a way to correct partitions when data trickles in after you've already processed them.

Decision justification

I partition and aggregate by event time (when it happened), not processing time, because business questions are about when things happened. To bound state and cost I use a watermark / grace period — accept lateness up to N hours/days, then close the window. For batch lakes I add a lookback reprocessing window: each run re-computes not just today but the last few days, so late records landing in older partitions get folded in idempotently. I pick the lateness bound from the actual observed distribution of delays, trading completeness against latency and cost.

Full answer

"First I separate event time from processing/ingestion time and key everything on event time. Then I decide a lateness policy from the data's real delay distribution — say 99% of events arrive within 6 hours."

  • Streaming: use a watermark — withWatermark("event_time", "6 hours"). The engine keeps window state open for 6 hours of event-time lag, then drops later stragglers, bounding memory.
  • Batch: use a lookback window. The daily run reprocesses, e.g., the trailing 3 days and overwrites those partitions idempotently, so late arrivals get absorbed without double-counting.
  • Reconciliation: for stragglers beyond the window, run a periodic full reconciliation (e.g., monthly close) that catches anything the grace period missed.
  • Make the trade-off explicit: a longer grace period = more complete but higher latency and cost (more state / more reprocessing). Short = fast but may undercount. The right number is a business decision.

⚑ Gotcha

There is no "correct" watermark — only a chosen trade-off. Too tight and you silently drop real events; too loose and state/cost balloons and results stay "not final" for too long. Derive it from the measured arrival-delay distribution, and document it as an SLA.

What not to say

Don't bucket data by processing time and call it done — that misattributes late events and is the most common correctness bug.

Don't say "just keep all window state forever" — unbounded state kills streaming jobs; the whole point of watermarks is to bound it.

Q07

How do you handle schema evolution without breaking downstream consumers?

schemacompatibilitycontracts
How to think about this

Schemas change — that's a given. The interviewer wants to know you have a discipline that lets producers evolve without paging every consumer. The core concept is backward/forward compatibility: additive changes (new nullable column) are safe; destructive ones (drop/rename/retype a column) break readers. Senior answers center on governing change, not just reacting to it.

Decision justification

I enforce backward-compatible, additive-only evolution by default: new columns are nullable with defaults; you never drop, rename, or retype in place. I justify this because consumers read at their own pace — a breaking change is effectively a coordinated multi-team deploy, which doesn't scale. For governed change I use a schema registry (Avro/Protobuf) with compatibility checks in CI, or table formats (Iceberg/Delta) that track schema evolution safely. When a truly breaking change is unavoidable, I version the dataset (v2 table/topic) and run both in parallel during migration rather than mutate in place.

Full answer

"My rule is additive and backward-compatible by default. New fields are optional/nullable so old consumers ignore them and new consumers use them. Dropping or renaming is a breaking change and gets handled as a versioned migration, not an in-place edit."

  • Compatibility modes — backward (new schema reads old data: add optional fields), forward (old schema reads new data), full (both). Pick one and enforce it.
  • Registry + CI gate. A schema registry validates each producer change against the rule and fails the build if it's incompatible — catching breakage before prod.
  • Table formats. Iceberg/Delta support safe column add/rename/reorder with metadata, decoupling logical schema from physical files.
  • Parquet/Glue: enable schema merging carefully; prefer explicit schema management over crawler-driven type guessing, which can flip a column's type and break jobs.
  • Breaking change protocol: publish v2 alongside v1, migrate consumers, deprecate v1 — never yank the rug.

"This ties into data contracts (see Q38): the schema + compatibility rule is the contract between producer and consumer, enforced in CI."

Q08

ETL vs ELT — when do you choose each?

etl-eltwarehousetrade-offs
How to think about this

This looks like a definition question but it's really about where transformation compute lives and why that's shifted. The interviewer wants you to connect the rise of ELT to cheap, elastic, separated cloud-warehouse compute (Snowflake, Redshift RA3, BigQuery). The mental model: ETL transforms before loading (compute outside the warehouse); ELT loads raw then transforms inside (compute = the warehouse engine). Modern cloud-native stacks lean ELT.

Decision justification

I default to ELT for cloud-warehouse analytics because: (1) you land raw and keep it, so new requirements re-transform existing data instead of re-extracting; (2) warehouse compute is elastic and SQL-based, so transformations are cheap to write, test (dbt), and scale; (3) it decouples ingestion speed from transformation logic. I keep ETL where transformation must happen before landing — heavy unstructured processing better suited to Spark, PII that must be masked/tokenized before it touches the warehouse, or when the target can't transform efficiently. For this role's Glue + Snowflake stack, the common pattern is "EL with Glue into the lake/Snowflake, then T with SQL/dbt" — a hybrid.

Full answer

"The difference is the order of Transform and Load, which really means where the compute runs."

  • ETL — transform in a separate engine (Spark/Glue) before loading. Historically necessary when warehouse storage/compute were expensive and coupled, so you only loaded clean, modeled data. Still right for: pre-load PII masking, complex non-SQL logic, and protecting an expensive target.
  • ELT — load raw into cheap storage, transform with the warehouse's own elastic compute (SQL/dbt). Wins on agility (raw is preserved, so re-modeling is free), testability, and scaling. The default for Snowflake/Redshift/BigQuery analytics.

"Why the shift? Cloud warehouses separated storage from compute and made compute elastic and pay-per-use. That removed the reason to pre-shrink data before loading. In practice I often run a hybrid: Glue does the heavy lift / format conversion (EL), then dbt-style SQL models inside Snowflake do the business transforms (T)."

Q09

Explain and justify the medallion (bronze/silver/gold) architecture.

lakehousearchitecturelayering
How to think about this

Medallion is just a disciplined naming for progressive refinement. The interviewer wants you to articulate why staging data in layers — each layer a clear contract on quality — beats one big transform. The mental model: each layer trades rawness for trust. Bronze = "exactly what arrived." Silver = "clean and conformed." Gold = "business-ready."

Decision justification

I separate layers because it gives me a replay point and a debugging boundary at every stage. Bronze being immutable means I can re-derive silver/gold whenever logic changes — no re-extraction. Putting the expensive cleansing/conforming in silver once, rather than in every gold mart, avoids duplicated logic and inconsistent definitions. Gold being narrow and aggregated keeps BI fast and cheap. The cost is more storage and more pipeline steps — worth it for traceability and trust, which is exactly what a platform-enablement team sells to other teams.

Full answer
  • Bronze (raw). Ingested as-is, append-only, schema-on-read, partitioned by load date. Full fidelity + audit trail. You never lose source information; this is your replay buffer.
  • Silver (cleansed/conformed). Deduplicated, typed, validated, joined into a clean canonical model. Business rules that are universally true live here. This is the trusted source most consumers should build on.
  • Gold (curated/serving). Aggregated, denormalized facts/dimensions or metrics marts, tuned for specific consumption — BI dashboards, ML features, exec reporting.

"The discipline is that quality increases left to right and each arrow is reproducible. If a gold metric looks wrong, I can trace it back through silver to the exact bronze records. If a definition changes, I re-run from bronze. It also lets different teams attach at the layer matching their trust/latency needs — data scientists may want silver, execs want gold."

Q10

How would you ingest changes from an operational DB using CDC?

cdcingestionreplication
How to think about this

CDC is "capture inserts/updates/deletes from a source DB and propagate them" — the interviewer wants to know you understand the three ways to do it and their trade-offs, and critically that log-based CDC is the gold standard because it captures deletes and doesn't load the source. The deeper signal: knowing that CDC turns a stream of changes into a current-state table requires an idempotent MERGE keyed on the primary key, ordered by a change sequence.

Decision justification

I prefer log-based CDC (read the DB transaction log — MySQL binlog, Postgres WAL — via Debezium/DMS) because it's low-impact on the source, captures deletes and every intermediate change, and gives ordered events. I avoid query-based CDC (polling WHERE updated_at > last_run) for anything serious because it misses deletes, misses intermediate updates between polls, and adds query load — though it's fine for simple append-only tables. To materialize current state I MERGE by primary key ordered by the log sequence number / commit timestamp, applying deletes as tombstones, which is idempotent and replay-safe.

Full answer

"There are three flavors. Log-based reads the DB's write-ahead/transaction log — this is what I'd choose: minimal source impact, captures deletes, preserves order. Tools: Debezium → Kafka, or AWS DMS. Trigger-based fires DB triggers into a shadow table — captures everything but adds write overhead and operational coupling to the source. Query/timestamp-based polls an updated_at column — simplest but misses deletes and any updates between polls."

"The change stream lands in bronze as an append-only log of events (op = insert/update/delete, pk, before/after, LSN). To get current state in silver I MERGE on the primary key, ordering by LSN so the latest change wins, and translate delete ops into row removals (or soft-delete flags). Because the MERGE is keyed and ordered, reprocessing the same events is idempotent."

"For the Redshift→Snowflake context, CDC is also how you keep the target in sync during a migration's dual-run phase."

How to think about this

Decision justification

Q13

Incremental vs full-refresh loads — how do you decide and implement?

How to think about this

This tests pragmatism about cost vs complexity vs correctness. Full refresh (reload everything) is simple and self-healing but doesn't scale. Incremental (only changed rows) scales but needs a reliable change-detection mechanism and idempotent merge. The interviewer wants you to pick based on data volume, change rate, and how reliably you can detect changes — and to know full-refresh is a perfectly good answer for small tables.

Decision justification

I use full refresh while the table is small enough to reload within the SLA window (think dimensions, config, reference data) because it's simple, has no drift risk, and self-heals any past mistake. I switch to incremental once full reload is too slow or expensive — large fact tables — accepting the added complexity of a watermark and idempotent MERGE. The deciding factors: absolute volume, daily change ratio, and whether I have a trustworthy change signal (CDC, an updated_at, or append-only event time). If change detection is unreliable, I lean full-refresh or add periodic full reconciliation to correct incremental drift.

Full answer
  • Full refresh: truncate + reload (or rebuild + atomic swap). Pros: dead simple, no state, self-healing — yesterday's bug vanishes on reload. Cons: cost and time grow with table size; impractical for billions of rows.
  • Incremental: load only new/changed rows since a high-water mark (max processed timestamp/LSN), then MERGE/upsert idempotently. Pros: scales, cheap per run. Cons: needs reliable change detection; risks drift if a change is missed; deletes need explicit handling.

"Implementation of incremental: persist the last watermark, extract WHERE updated_at > watermark (or consume CDC), MERGE on the key, then advance the watermark only after a successful commit so a failure re-pulls rather than skips. I also schedule a periodic full reconciliation (weekly/monthly) to catch any drift from missed updates or deletes — belt and suspenders."

Q14

How do you design a deduplication strategy at scale?

dedupcorrectnessspark
How to think about this

Duplicates are inevitable with at-least-once sources, retries, and CDC. The interviewer wants two things: a clear definition of what "duplicate" means (exact-copy vs same-business-key-different-version) and a method that scales without an O(n²) comparison. The senior framing: dedup is "pick the right one row per key," usually via a windowed rank on a deterministic key + ordering, done within partitions to avoid massive shuffles.

Decision justification

I dedup on a stable business/event key rather than whole-row equality, because the same logical record often arrives with a later timestamp or enriched fields and I want the latest, not an arbitrary copy. At scale I use a window function (row_number() over (partition by key order by event_time desc)) keeping rn = 1 — it's set-based and lets Spark partition the work by key. I prefer this over DISTINCT (which only removes exact duplicates and can't pick a winner) and over self-joins (O(n²)). For streaming/incremental, I keep a seen-keys state or use the table-format MERGE so duplicates across batches are also caught.

Full answer

"First I define duplicate precisely. If it's exact byte-for-byte copies, DISTINCT/dropDuplicates suffices. Usually it's same key, possibly different versions — then I need to choose a winner, typically the latest by event time or highest sequence number."

  • Batch, pick-latest: row_number() partitioned by the dedup key, ordered by recency, filter to rank 1. Set-based, scales by key, no quadratic comparisons.
  • Across batches (incremental/streaming): MERGE into the target on the key so a record seen yesterday and again today resolves to one row; or maintain a watermarked dropDuplicates state in Structured Streaming bounded by event time.
  • Generate a stable dedup key upstream. If the source lacks a natural id, derive a deterministic hash of the identifying fields so the same logical event always hashes identically.
  • Skew awareness: if one key is hot, the window's per-key partition can skew — handle as in Q19.

  .withColumn("rn", row_number().over(w))
  .where(col("rn") == 1)          # latest version of each event_id
  .drop("rn"))

Q16

How do Glue job bookmarks work, and what are the pitfalls?

How to think about this

Bookmarks are Glue's built-in incremental mechanism, and the interviewer wants to know you can use them and name where they bite. The model: Glue persists state about what it already read (by file timestamp/path or a bookmark key) so the next run processes only new data. The senior signal is awareness that bookmarks are convenient but opaque state — and that opacity causes the classic production incidents.

Decision justification

I use bookmarks for straightforward append-only S3 ingestion where "process new files since last run" is exactly the need — they remove hand-rolled watermark code. I'm cautious with them when correctness is critical because the state is managed by Glue and not visible in my data, which makes debugging "why did it skip/reprocess?" hard. For complex incremental logic I often prefer an explicit watermark in a control table (Q13) that I own and can inspect. Whatever I choose, I keep writes idempotent so a bookmark reset/rewind doesn't duplicate data.

Full answer

"A bookmark is server-side state keyed to the job that records what's been processed — for S3 sources it tracks files by path and modification time; for JDBC it can track a bookmark column. You enable it, call job.commit() at the end, and each run picks up only new input. It's incremental processing without writing your own high-water-mark logic."

Pitfalls I watch for:

  • Opaque state. You can't easily see what the bookmark thinks it processed; troubleshooting skipped/duplicated data is painful.
  • Must call job.commit(). Forget it and the bookmark never advances — every run reprocesses everything.
  • Late/modified files. If files are rewritten or arrive with older timestamps, bookmarks can skip them (they look "already processed").
  • Resets are blunt. Resetting reprocesses from scratch; without idempotent writes that means duplicates.
  • Not a substitute for idempotency. Bookmarks reduce reads, they don't make your writes safe — still overwrite partitions or MERGE.

Q17

Walk me through the Spark execution model.

How to think about this

This is the foundation that makes every other Spark answer credible. The interviewer wants the hierarchy and the lazy-evaluation insight: driver plans, executors do work; nothing runs until an action; the boundary between stages is a shuffle. If you understand narrow vs wide transformations, you can explain performance for the rest of the interview.

Decision justification

I frame Spark performance around the shuffle boundary because that's where time and money go. Knowing that narrow transformations (map, filter) pipeline within a stage while wide ones (groupBy, join, distinct) force a shuffle and a new stage tells me exactly where to optimize: minimize and shrink shuffles. This single mental model — driver plans a DAG, splits it at shuffles into stages, each stage = parallel tasks (one per partition) — is what I reason from when tuning.

Full answer
  • Driver — runs your program, builds the logical plan, asks Catalyst to optimize it, and schedules work. The brain. Single point; don't collect() huge data to it.
  • Executors — worker JVMs that run tasks and hold data partitions in memory/disk. The muscle; you scale these.
  • Lazy evaluation — transformations (map, filter, join) just build a DAG; nothing executes until an action (count, write, collect). This lets Catalyst optimize the whole chain (e.g., push filters down, prune columns) before running.
  • Job → Stages → Tasks. An action triggers a job. The DAG is cut into stages at shuffle boundaries. Each stage runs as tasks — one per partition — in parallel across executors.
  • Narrow vs wide. Narrow (map/filter): each output partition depends on one input partition — no data movement, pipelined. Wide (groupBy/join/distinct): output partitions depend on many inputs — requires a shuffle (network + disk), which ends one stage and starts the next.

Q18

What is a shuffle, and how do you minimize shuffles?

sparkperformanceshuffle
How to think about this

A shuffle is the most expensive thing Spark does, so this question tests whether you can make jobs fast. The model: a shuffle redistributes data across the cluster by key — writing to disk, sending over the network, re-reading — so executors can group/join matching keys together. The interviewer wants you to (a) explain why it's costly and (b) list concrete tactics to avoid or shrink it.

Decision justification

I attack shuffles because they dominate cost: disk I/O + network + serialization, all of which dwarf in-memory compute. My priority order: (1) eliminate the shuffle (broadcast the small side of a join so the big side never moves); (2) shrink it (filter and project before the wide op so less data shuffles — predicate/column pushdown); (3) right-size it (tune shuffle.partitions / rely on AQE so you don't get thousands of tiny tasks or a few giant ones); (4) reuse it (cache after an expensive shuffle if reused). I justify broadcast-first because removing the shuffle entirely beats optimizing it.

Full answer

"A shuffle happens on wide transformations — joins, groupBy, distinct, repartition — where rows must be regrouped by key across executors. Spark writes shuffle files to local disk, transfers them over the network, and the next stage reads them. That's why it's the costliest operation and the usual reason a job is slow."

How I minimize it:

  • Broadcast joins — if one table is small (< ~10–100MB), broadcast it to every executor so the large table isn't shuffled at all.
  • Filter and select early — push predicates and column pruning before the shuffle so fewer/narrower rows move. Columnar formats + Catalyst do much of this automatically.
  • Avoid needless repartition — don't repartition() unless you must; it's a full shuffle. Use coalesce() to reduce partitions without a shuffle.
  • Tune shuffle partitions / enable AQE — the default 200 is often wrong; Adaptive Query Execution coalesces small shuffle partitions and handles skew at runtime.
  • Pre-bucket hot join keys so repeated joins skip the shuffle.
  • Cache a DataFrame that's shuffled once and reused many times.
Code / diagram
Broadcast the small side to eliminate a shuffle
from pyspark.sql.functions import broadcast

# big_fact stays put; small_dim is shipped to every executor
result = big_fact.join(broadcast(small_dim), "product_id")

# Enable Adaptive Query Execution (right-sizes shuffle partitions, fixes skew)
spark.conf.set("spark.sql.adaptive.enabled", "true")
spark.conf.set("spark.sql.adaptive.coalescePartitions.enabled", "true")
⚑ Remember this

The cheapest shuffle is the one that never happens. Reach for a broadcast join first; only when neither side is small do you optimize the shuffle itself (early filtering, AQE, partition tuning).

What not to say

Don't say "just add more executors" as the fix — more parallelism doesn't reduce the data being shuffled; it can even worsen network pressure.

Don't reach for repartition() casually — it's itself a full shuffle. Use coalesce() to shrink partition count cheaply.

Q19

How do you diagnose and fix data skew in Spark?

sparkskewdebugging
How to think about this

Skew is the most common "my job is mysteriously slow / one task runs forever" problem, so this tests real operational experience. The mental model: Spark parallelizes by key-partition, so if one key (e.g., customer_id = NULL or a whale account) holds most rows, one task does most of the work while others finish and idle — a straggler. The interviewer wants you to diagnose from the Spark UI and fix with salting / AQE.

Decision justification

My first move is enable AQE skew-join handling because it fixes the common case automatically at runtime (it detects oversized partitions and splits them) with zero code change — cheapest possible fix. When AQE isn't enough or the skew is in an aggregation, I salt the hot key: add a random suffix to spread one mega-key across N partitions, aggregate, then combine — trading a bit of extra compute for balanced parallelism. I isolate NULL/default keys separately because they're a frequent skew culprit and often shouldn't be joined at all. I justify diagnosing first (Spark UI: one task with huge input/duration) so I fix the actual skewed stage, not guess.

Full answer

"Diagnose first: in the Spark UI, a skewed stage shows a few tasks with far larger input size / shuffle read / duration than the median — the max-vs-75th-percentile gap is the tell. That straggler is one overloaded partition."

Fixes, in order of preference:

  • AQE skew join — spark.sql.adaptive.skewJoin.enabled=true. Spark splits oversized partitions at runtime. Free, try first.
  • Salting — append a random integer (0..N) to the hot key on both sides, join/group on the salted key to spread the load across N tasks, then aggregate away the salt. Best for a known dominant key.
  • Isolate NULL/sentinel keys — filter them out or handle separately; NULL join keys collapse into one giant partition and usually shouldn't join anyway.
  • Broadcast — if the non-skewed side is small, broadcasting sidesteps the shuffle entirely (no key-partitioning, no skew).
  • Separate hot keys — process whale keys in their own job path tuned for them.

Q20

The small-files problem — causes and fixes.

How to think about this

This tests practical lake hygiene. The interviewer wants you to know why millions of tiny files are slow (per-file overhead: S3 listing, opening, task scheduling, metadata — fixed cost per file that dominates when files are small) and the handful of standard fixes. It's a favorite because it's ubiquitous and the cause is usually self-inflicted (over-partitioning, too many output tasks, streaming microbatches).

Decision justification

I target a file size of ~128MB–1GB because that amortizes per-file overhead and matches HDFS/Parquet block sizing and Spark's read-split logic. To hit it on write I control output partition count: coalesce() to reduce file count without a shuffle, or repartition() when I need balanced sizes and can afford the shuffle. I avoid over-partitioning the table (Q5) since that's the root cause. For append/streaming workloads that inevitably emit small files, I run a scheduled compaction job (or use a table format's OPTIMIZE/compaction) to bin-pack them. The justification: read performance and metastore health are worth a little write-time effort.

Full answer

"Every file carries fixed overhead — an S3 list/open call, a metadata entry, and a Spark task to read it. When files are tiny, that overhead dominates and you spend all your time on bookkeeping instead of data. Symptoms: slow listing, slow query planning, thousands of tiny tasks, metastore bloat."

Common causes: over-partitioning (high-cardinality partition column), too many shuffle/output partitions, and streaming/incremental jobs writing a few rows per microbatch.

Fixes:

  • Control writer parallelism. df.coalesce(n) before write to merge output partitions without a shuffle; repartition(n) when you need even sizes (costs a shuffle).

  • Scheduled compaction. Periodically read small files and rewrite into fewer large ones; with Iceberg/Delta/Hudi use their built-in OPTIMIZE/compaction + vacuum.
  • Tune target file size (e.g., Spark maxRecordsPerFile, or table-format target-file-size) so the engine packs files for you.

Q21

Spark joins: broadcast vs sort-merge vs shuffle-hash — when does each win?

How to think about this

Joins are where Spark spends most of its time, so the interviewer wants you to know the three physical join strategies and the data-size conditions that pick each. The model is simple: if one side is small enough to fit in memory, broadcast it and skip the shuffle; if both are large, you must shuffle both by key — Spark sorts them and merges (sort-merge). Knowing how to force or prevent a broadcast is the applied signal.

Decision justification

I want a broadcast hash join whenever one side is small (under spark.sql.autoBroadcastJoinThreshold, default 10MB, but I'll raise it or hint explicitly up to ~100s of MB if the executors have memory) because it eliminates the big side's shuffle entirely — the single biggest join speedup. When both sides are large I accept a sort-merge join (Spark's default for big-big) and focus on shrinking the shuffle (filter early, handle skew). Shuffle-hash is a niche middle ground — one side fits in memory per-partition after shuffling — that Spark rarely picks by default. I justify hinting broadcasts because Spark's size estimate is often wrong for derived/filtered tables.

Full answer
  • Broadcast hash join. Ship the small table to every executor; each joins its slice of the big table locally — no shuffle of the big side. Fastest. Condition: small side fits comfortably in executor memory. The optimization you want most often.
  • Sort-merge join. Both sides shuffled by key, sorted, then merged. Spark's default when both tables are large. Robust and scalable, but pays the full double shuffle + sort.
  • Shuffle-hash join. Both shuffled by key, then a hash table built on one side per partition (no sort). Used rarely — when one side is medium and sorting is undesirable; disabled by default in favor of sort-merge's stability.

"Spark auto-broadcasts under the threshold, but its size estimates can be off — especially after filters or for tables without stats — so I'll explicitly broadcast()-hint when I know a side is small, or raise the threshold. Conversely, if a broadcast is OOMing executors, I'll disable it. And I always check whether AQE can convert a sort-merge to a broadcast at runtime once actual sizes are known."

How do you keep the Glue Catalog consistent and partitions in sync?

How to think about this

Decision justification

  • MSCK REPAIR TABLE — bulk-discovers Hive-style partitions; handy for one-off fixes but doesn't scale to millions and is slow.

Q24

How do you design a robust Airflow DAG?

airflowreliabilitydesign
How to think about this

The interviewer wants the properties of a production-grade DAG, not Airflow trivia. The three pillars: tasks are idempotent (rerun-safe), atomic (do one thing, fail cleanly), and parameterized by the logical date (so backfills and reruns are deterministic). Everything else — retries, alerting, granularity — supports those. This connects directly to Q3/Q4.

Decision justification

I make each task idempotent and atomic so the scheduler can retry freely and a partial failure never corrupts state — this is what lets Airflow's at-least-once execution be safe. I keep tasks at a sensible granularity: one logical step each (extract / transform / load), so failures are isolated and reruns are cheap — but not so fine-grained that scheduler overhead and XCom plumbing dominate. I template everything on {{ ds }} (logical date) rather than runtime now() so a run for any date is reproducible. And I treat the DAG as orchestration only — it triggers Glue/Spark/SQL, it doesn't do heavy data processing on the worker.

Full answer
  • Idempotent tasks. A retried/re-run task produces the same result — overwrite a partition or MERGE, never blind-append. The scheduler will retry; design for it.
  • Atomic + single-purpose. Each task does one thing and either fully succeeds or fully fails (write to temp, atomic swap). No half-written outputs on failure.
  • Parameterize by logical date. Use {{ ds }}/{{ data_interval_start }} templating; never datetime.now() in task logic — that breaks backfills and reruns.
  • Right granularity. Split so failures isolate and restart cheaply, but avoid thousands of trivial tasks that bloat the scheduler.
  • Orchestrate, don't compute. The worker submits a Glue/EMR/Snowflake job and waits; heavy lifting runs on the data engine, not the Airflow worker.
  • Retries + alerting + SLAs. Configurable retries with backoff for transient failures, on_failure_callback to alert, and SLA miss detection for lateness.
  • No top-level heavy code. The DAG file is parsed constantly by the scheduler — keep imports/logic out of the module top level or you slow the whole scheduler.

Don't propose doing the actual data crunching inside a PythonOperator on the worker — Airflow orchestrates; it's not a compute engine.

Q25

Explain Airflow scheduling: logical/execution date, catchup, and backfill.

How to think about this

This is a famous Airflow gotcha, and getting it right signals real hands-on experience. The key insight: a DAG run is labeled with the start of the interval it covers, but fires at the end of that interval. So the run "for June 7th" actually executes early June 8th. Interviewers love this because it trips up people who've only read docs.

Decision justification

I lean on the logical date (a.k.a. data_interval) as the contract for which data slice a run processes, not as wall-clock time, because that's what makes runs idempotent and backfillable. I enable catchup deliberately: on for tables where every historical interval must be filled (it auto-backfills from start_date); off for jobs where only "latest" matters and I don't want a flood of historical runs on deploy. I justify treating the interval-end firing as a feature: it guarantees the period's data is complete before the run starts.

Full answer

"Airflow schedules intervals, not instants. For a daily DAG, the run with logical date 2026-06-07 covers the data interval June 7 00:00 → June 8 00:00 and executes at the end of that window — just after midnight on the 8th. The logical date tells the task which day's data to process; that's why you template {{ ds }} = 2026-06-07 to read June 7's partition."

  • Logical date / data_interval — the period the run is responsible for. The deterministic input to your idempotent task.
  • Fires at interval end — so the data for that period is complete before processing. (Newer Airflow uses data_interval_start/end to make this explicit and less confusing than the legacy "execution_date" name.)
  • Catchup — if catchup=True, when you deploy or unpause, Airflow schedules a run for every missed interval back to start_date. That's automatic backfill; set catchup=False to only run from now.
  • Backfill — manually (or via catchup) replaying historical intervals. Safe precisely because each run is parameterized by its logical date and writes idempotently (Q4).

Q26

Sensors vs deferrable operators vs event-driven — how do you avoid worker starvation?

How to think about this

This tests whether you've hit Airflow's classic scaling wall: sensors that block worker slots while waiting. If you have 100 sensors polling for files and only 96 worker slots, the whole cluster deadlocks waiting on itself. The interviewer wants you to know the evolution — poke mode → reschedule mode → deferrable operators → fully event-driven — and why each is better.

Decision justification

I avoid naive poke-mode sensors for anything long-waiting because each one occupies a worker slot the entire time, and a pile of them starves the cluster. I prefer deferrable operators / the triggerer, which release the worker slot and hand the wait to a lightweight async process — thousands can wait on one triggerer. Better still, when possible I go fully event-driven (S3 event → SNS/SQS/Lambda → trigger the DAG via the API or a Dataset), so nothing polls at all. The justification is resource efficiency: waiting should cost near-zero, not a whole worker.

Full answer
  • Sensor, poke mode (old default). Wakes up, checks a condition, sleeps, repeats — holding a worker slot the whole time. Fine for a few short waits; deadly at scale (worker starvation).
  • Sensor, reschedule mode. Between checks it releases the slot and is re-queued — frees the worker during sleeps. Better, but still re-occupies a slot on each poke and has scheduling overhead.
  • Deferrable operators (async + triggerer). The task defers — releases its worker slot entirely and registers a trigger handled by the async triggerer process. One triggerer can watch thousands of conditions cheaply. The modern answer for waiting.
  • Event-driven. Don't wait at all — let the upstream event start the work: S3 event → Lambda/SQS → trigger DAG, or Airflow Datasets so a DAG runs when its input dataset is updated. Lowest latency, no polling.

"So my default for a 'wait for file/upstream' need is a deferrable sensor; and where the platform supports it, I push to true event-driven triggering to remove polling latency and cost entirely."

  • Restartability. Because each task is idempotent + atomic, on failure I fix the root cause and clear/resume from the failed task — upstream successes are preserved. No full re-run, no duplicate data.

Q30

Explain Redshift's architecture and how you model for performance.

redshiftmpptuning
How to think about this

Redshift is a shared-nothing MPP database, and almost all its performance comes from two physical-design choices: the distribution key (which node a row lives on) and the sort key (the order within a node). The interviewer wants to see you reason about data placement — co-locating joined data to avoid network shuffles, and ordering data so the engine skips blocks. If you understand dist + sort, you understand Redshift.

Decision justification

I choose the distribution key to co-locate rows that join, because in MPP the expensive operation is redistributing data across nodes to perform a join. If fact.customer_id and dim.customer_id share a dist key, the join is local — no network. I use DISTSTYLE ALL for small dimensions (replicate to every node, so any join is local) and EVEN when there's no natural join key (to avoid skew). I choose the sort key on the column queries filter/range on (usually a date) so Redshift's zone maps skip blocks. I justify these as the highest-leverage decisions because a bad dist key causes broadcast/redistribution on every query, and a good one makes joins effectively free.

Full answer

"Redshift is columnar MPP: a leader node plans queries; compute nodes are divided into slices that process data in parallel, shared-nothing. Performance is dominated by how data is physically laid out across and within slices."

  • Distribution style/key — controls which slice a row goes to. KEY: hash on a column so rows with the same value co-locate (pick the common join key) → local joins. ALL: replicate the whole table to every node (small dimensions) → every join local. EVEN: round-robin (no good key) → balanced but joins may redistribute. AUTO: Redshift decides.
  • Sort key — physical row order within a slice. Redshift keeps per-block min/max (zone maps); a query filtering on the sort key skips blocks that can't match. Choose the column you filter/range-scan on most (commonly date).
  • Maintenance — VACUUM reclaims space and re-sorts after big DML; ANALYZE refreshes stats so the planner picks good plans. (RA3/auto features reduce manual need.)
  • Avoid skew — a dist key with lopsided value distribution overloads one slice (same idea as Spark skew, Q19).

  sale_id bigint, customer_id bigint, sale_ts timestamp, amount numeric

⚑ Remember this

In MPP, the enemy is data movement between nodes. The distribution key exists to make joins local; the sort key exists to make scans skip blocks. Get those two right and you've done 80% of Redshift tuning.

What not to say

Don't say "just add an index" — Redshift isn't a row-store; there are no secondary indexes. Sort keys + zone maps are the equivalent.

Don't pick a high-skew or rarely-joined column as the dist key — you'll either overload a slice or redistribute on every join.

Q31

Explain Snowflake's architecture and what makes it different.

How to think about this

Snowflake's whole pitch is separation of storage and compute, with automatic micro-partitioning instead of manual dist/sort keys. The interviewer wants you to contrast its cloud-native, hands-off model against Redshift's tunable MPP. Key concepts: micro-partitions + pruning (automatic), virtual warehouses (independent elastic compute), and the three-layer architecture (storage / compute / cloud services).

Decision justification

I value Snowflake's storage/compute separation because it lets independent teams run their own virtual warehouses against the same data without contention, and scale compute up/down (or to zero) per workload — you pay only for what runs. I rely on automatic micro-partitioning and pruning rather than hand-tuning dist/sort keys, which removes a whole category of DBA work (the trade is less manual control). I add clustering keys only on very large tables whose natural ingestion order doesn't match query filters, because clustering costs credits to maintain and most tables don't need it. The justification: Snowflake optimizes for low operational overhead and elasticity — ideal when many teams self-serve, which matches a platform-enablement mandate.

Full answer

"Snowflake has three decoupled layers:"

  • Storage — data sits in cloud object storage as immutable, compressed, columnar micro-partitions (~50–500MB), each with rich metadata (per-column min/max). Snowflake automatically prunes partitions that can't match a query — no manual partitioning needed.
  • Compute (virtual warehouses) — independent MPP compute clusters you size (XS…6XL) and start/stop. Multiple warehouses read the same storage without contending; you isolate workloads (ETL vs BI) and scale each independently. Multi-cluster warehouses auto-scale out for concurrency.
  • Cloud services — the brain: query optimization, metadata, security, transactions, and the result cache.

"Differentiators: true storage/compute separation (Redshift approached this with RA3 but Snowflake was built for it), automatic micro-partition pruning instead of dist/sort tuning, near-instant elastic scaling, plus zero-copy cloning and time travel (query/restore past states cheaply via metadata). The cost is less low-level control and a credit-based pricing model you must actively manage."

Redshift vs Snowflake — key differences and migration considerations.

How to think about this

This is the central JD question. Show you understand why a team migrates and what breaks if you treat it as a lift-and-shift.

Decision justification

I frame the choice around operational model and concurrency. Q34

How do you validate data parity between source and target during migration?

How to think about this

This is the trust-building half of Q33. The interviewer wants a tiered validation strategy that's both thorough and affordable — you can't diff billions of rows one-by-one across two systems every day. The signal: layered checks from cheap-and-broad (counts) to expensive-and-precise (checksums/sampled diffs), automated and run continuously during the parallel period.

Decision justification

I use a tiered approach because validation cost and precision trade off: cheap checks run constantly to catch gross errors; expensive ones run periodically or on samples to catch subtle ones. Row counts and key aggregates (sum/min/max/distinct on important columns) catch most issues for near-zero cost. Hash/checksum of normalized rows catches value-level corruption that aggregates miss (e.g., two offsetting errors that net to the same sum). Sampled row-by-row diffs give confidence without full-scan cost. I automate it per table per run and gate cutover on a sustained green window, because one-time validation can't catch ongoing pipeline divergence.

Full answer
  • Tier 1 — Row counts. Per table, per partition/day. Cheapest; catches missing/extra rows immediately. Run every cycle.
  • Tier 2 — Aggregate checks. SUM, MIN/MAX, COUNT(DISTINCT), null counts on business-critical columns. Catches value drift cheaply. (But two compensating errors can net out — hence Tier 3.)
  • Tier 3 — Checksums / hashing. Hash each row's normalized concatenation, aggregate the hashes per partition, compare. Detects value-level differences aggregates miss. Beware type/format/precision/ordering mismatches that cause false positives — normalize first.
  • Tier 4 — Sampled row diffs. Pull a statistical sample (or all rows for a few key dates) and diff column-by-column to localize discrepancies for debugging.
  • Automate & trend. Run as a reconciliation job each cycle, log results, dashboard pass/fail, and only cut a workload over after it's green for a sustained window. Investigate every mismatch — often it's a legitimate dialect/precision difference, not data loss.

How do you build database CI/CD for safe schema changes?

How to think about this

The JD explicitly calls out "database CI/CD." The interviewer wants you to treat schema as version-controlled code with automated, tested, repeatable deployments — not someone running ad-hoc ALTER statements in prod. Core concepts: migrations in source control, applied in order, idempotently, promoted through environments, with backward-compatible (expand/contract) changes so deploys don't break running consumers.

Decision justification

I treat schema changes as versioned migration scripts in git (Flyway/Liquibase/dbt) because it gives history, review, and reproducibility — the same reasons we version application code. I apply them via automated pipelines through dev→staging→prod so every environment is consistent and changes are tested before prod. I insist on expand/contract (backward-compatible) changes — add the new column/table, deploy code that writes both, migrate, then remove the old — so a schema change never breaks in-flight readers and is rollback-safe. I justify the discipline because in a multi-team platform, an unreviewed prod ALTER can break dozens of downstream consumers silently.

Full answer

  • Expand/contract pattern. Backward-compatible rollout: (1) expand — add new column/table (nullable); (2) deploy code that dual-writes/reads; (3) backfill; (4) contract — drop the old once nothing uses it. Never rename-in-place a column consumers depend on (Q7).

  • Environment parity. The point is that dev/staging/prod schemas are identical and reproducible — a goal the JD names directly ("environment consistency").

How to think about this

Q37

How do you build a data-quality framework?

How to think about this

The interviewer wants a structured framework, not "I'd add some checks." Two axes: what you check (the DQ dimensions) and where/how you enforce it (gates between layers, fail vs warn vs quarantine). The senior signal is treating DQ as a first-class, automated, layered part of the pipeline with clear severity policy — and knowing that where you catch a problem matters as much as that you catch it.

Decision justification

I organize checks around the standard DQ dimensions (completeness, accuracy, consistency, timeliness, uniqueness, validity) so coverage is systematic rather than ad hoc. I enforce at layer boundaries — validate on ingest (reject bad data early, cheaply) and gate before serving (never publish bad data to consumers) — because catching errors late, after they've propagated into marts and dashboards, is exponentially more expensive. I assign severity: hard failures stop the pipeline (circuit-breaker) for critical violations; soft warnings alert but proceed for minor ones; bad rows get quarantined rather than dropped or allowed to fail the whole run. I justify automation + a tool (Great Expectations / Deequ / dbt tests) because manual QA doesn't scale and isn't repeatable.

Full answer

"I'd frame it on the DQ dimensions and enforce them automatically at the right points."

  • Dimensions (what to check): Completeness (nulls, missing rows/partitions), Accuracy (values match reality / reference), Consistency (cross-system/cross-table agreement, referential integrity), Timeliness (freshness/on-time arrival), Uniqueness (no unexpected dupes), Validity (types, ranges, formats, allowed values).
  • Where to enforce: On ingest — validate incoming data, reject/quarantine the bad early. Between layers — silver-to-gold gates so only clean data is promoted. Before serving — a final gate so consumers never see broken data.
  • Severity policy: critical checks fail closed (circuit-breaker stops publish); warnings alert but continue; suspect rows go to a quarantine table for inspection instead of silently dropping or blowing up the run.
  • Automation & tooling: declarative expectations (Great Expectations, AWS Deequ, dbt tests) versioned alongside the pipeline; results logged and dashboarded so quality is trended, not just pass/fail in the moment.

What are data contracts and how do they prevent breakage?

How to think about this

Data contracts are a hot, senior-level topic. The interviewer wants you to frame it as "shift-left" governance: catch breaking changes at the producer's CI, before bad data is ever emitted, rather than firefighting downstream.

Decision justification

I advocate data contracts because the default failure mode of data platforms is silent upstream breakage: a producer renames a column or changes a unit, and three teams' dashboards break hours later with no warning. Full answer

"A data contract is an explicit, machine-checkable agreement between the team producing a dataset and the teams consuming it. Compatible changes (additive, nullable — Q7) pass; breaking ones force an explicit, versioned migration and consumer coordination."

Q40

It's 3am and a critical pipeline failed / data is late. Walk me through your response.

How to think about this

This is a behavioral-meets-operational question testing calm, structured incident handling. The interviewer wants a clear sequence: assess severity → communicate → mitigate before you perfectly diagnose → recover → root-cause later. The biggest signal is prioritizing stakeholder communication and restoring service over finding the perfect root cause first — and never making a panicked change that worsens things.

Decision justification

I triage severity first because it dictates everything — a late internal dev table is not a customer-facing revenue feed, and over- or under-reacting both cost trust. I communicate early to stakeholders (even just "aware, investigating, ETA to follow") because in data, a known delay is far less damaging than people unknowingly making decisions on stale/missing data.   2. Contain. If bad data could propagate, stop the publish (circuit-breaker) so downstream consumers don't ingest corruption — a delay is recoverable; published bad data is much worse.

Q41

How do you detect and handle data drift / silent corruption?

How to think about this

"Silent" is the operative word: no error, no failed job — just data that's gradually or suddenly wrong. The interviewer wants to know you can catch problems that don't throw exceptions: distribution shifts, creeping null rates, volume anomalies, a unit change upstream. The mental model is statistical monitoring against a baseline — you can't catch drift you don't profile.

Decision justification

I detect drift by profiling data continuously and comparing against an expected baseline, because silent corruption by definition won't trigger a failure — only a deviation in the data's statistics reveals it. I monitor volume, null rates, cardinality, value distributions, and key aggregates over time, alerting on anomalous change. I distinguish data drift (the world genuinely changed — a real trend) from corruption (a pipeline/source bug) by cross-checking against source reconciliation and recent deploys/upstream changes. I justify the investment because these are the incidents that erode trust most — they ship wrong numbers for days before anyone notices, and the fix often requires restating history.

Full answer

"Silent corruption won't fail a job, so I monitor the data's statistical profile against a baseline and alert on deviations."

  • Volume anomalies: row counts suddenly half or double → partial load, dropped partition, or accidental dupe.
  • Null / completeness drift: a column's null rate jumps from 1% to 30% → upstream schema or mapping change.
  • Distribution shift: mean/median/min/max/cardinality of key columns move outside expected bands → unit change, currency change, bad join, corrupted source. (Statistical tests or simple threshold bands on a rolling baseline.)
  • Schema drift: new/missing/retyped columns slipping through.
  • Reconciliation: periodically re-check totals against the source of truth to catch divergence the in-pipeline checks miss.

"When I detect it: contain (hold publish if downstream isn't yet poisoned), determine whether it's real drift (legitimate change → update the baseline/expectation) or corruption (bug → fix and backfill the affected window), then add a check so this specific failure can't recur silently. Q44

Caching strategies and cache invalidation — how do you reason about them?

cachingperformancetrade-offs
How to think about this

Caching is a trade of freshness for speed/cost, and the interviewer wants you to name that trade and pick a strategy from the read/write pattern and staleness tolerance. The famous line — "the two hard things are cache invalidation and naming things" — is true: the strategy is easy; keeping the cache correct is the hard part. In data systems, materialized aggregates and warehouse result caches are caching too.

Decision justification

I default to cache-aside (lazy loading) for read-heavy workloads because it's simple, only caches what's actually requested, and tolerates cache failures gracefully (miss → read DB). I add a TTL as a backstop so staleness is bounded even if explicit invalidation misses — this is the pragmatic answer to invalidation being hard. I choose write-through when reads must never be stale (the cache is updated synchronously on write, at the cost of write latency), and write-back only when write throughput dominates and I can tolerate the durability risk of buffered writes. I justify TTL-plus-event-invalidation as belt-and-suspenders: events keep it fresh, TTL caps the damage when an event is missed.

Full answer
  • Cache-aside (lazy). App checks cache; on miss, reads DB and populates cache. Simplest, resilient to cache outages, caches only hot data. Downside: first request is a miss, and writes must invalidate. Most common.
  • Write-through. Writes go to cache and DB synchronously. Cache always fresh; reads never stale. Cost: higher write latency, caches data that may never be read.
  • Write-back (write-behind). Write to cache, flush to DB asynchronously. Fast writes, absorbs spikes; risk: data loss if cache dies before flush. For write-heavy, loss-tolerant cases.
  • Invalidation. The hard part. Options: TTL (expire after N seconds — bounded staleness, dead simple), event-based (invalidate on write — fresh but easy to miss a path), or both. Eviction (LRU/LFU) handles capacity separately from correctness.
  • In data systems: materialized views / pre-aggregated marts are caches of query results; Snowflake's result cache and warehouse cache are caching layers — same freshness-vs-speed trade.

"I'd choose based on read/write ratio and staleness tolerance, and almost always add a TTL as a safety net because perfect invalidation is the thing nobody gets 100% right."

Q45

Kafka / message queues — partitions, consumer groups, delivery guarantees, and when to use them.

How to think about this

The interviewer wants to know why you'd put a queue/log in a design (decoupling, buffering, fan-out) and that you understand the mechanics that everyone gets fuzzy on: partitions = parallelism + ordering unit, consumer groups = scaling reads, and the delivery-guarantee spectrum. Kafka specifically is a distributed log (replayable), not just a queue — that distinction matters.

Decision justification

I introduce a log/queue to decouple producers from consumers — so a slow or down consumer doesn't block producers, traffic spikes get buffered, and multiple independent consumers can read the same stream. I choose Kafka when I need replay, high throughput, and ordered partitioned streams (it retains the log), versus a traditional queue (SQS/RabbitMQ) for simpler task distribution. I design partitioning by the key whose order I must preserve (ordering is only guaranteed within a partition), and size partitions for the parallelism I need (one consumer per partition per group max). For guarantees I default to at-least-once + idempotent consumers (Q3) because true exactly-once is costly and idempotency is more robust.

Full answer
  • Why a queue/log: decoupling (producer/consumer scale and fail independently), buffering/backpressure (absorb spikes), and fan-out (many consumers, one stream).
  • Partitions: a topic is split into partitions — the unit of parallelism and of ordering. Order is guaranteed only within a partition, so you partition by the key you need ordered (e.g., user_id). More partitions = more parallelism but more overhead.
  • Consumer groups: each partition is consumed by exactly one consumer within a group, so a group scales horizontally up to the partition count. Different groups each get the full stream independently (fan-out).
  • Offsets & replay: Kafka is a retained log — consumers track an offset and can replay from any point. That's what enables reprocessing (Kappa, Q2) and recovery.
  • Delivery guarantees: at-most-once (commit offset before processing — may lose), at-least-once (process then commit — may duplicate; the common default), exactly-once (transactions/idempotent producer — possible in Kafka but costlier). Pair at-least-once with idempotent consumers for effectively-once.

Q46

CAP theorem and consistency models — what are the practical implications?

How to think about this

CAP is widely mis-stated, so saying it precisely is a strong signal. The accurate framing: when a network partition happens, you must choose between consistency and availability — you don't "pick 2 of 3" in normal operation. The interviewer wants the practical upshot: which kind of system tolerates stale reads (AP, eventual consistency) vs must always be correct (CP), and how that drives database choice.

Decision justification

I choose consistency vs availability based on the cost of being wrong vs the cost of being down, for the specific operation. Money movement, inventory decrement, unique-username — I pick CP / strong consistency because a stale or conflicting read causes real harm; I accept that during a partition those operations may be unavailable. Likes, view counts, product catalogs, analytics — I pick AP / eventual consistency because availability matters more than momentary staleness, and the data converges. I cite PACELC as the more complete model: even without partitions, you trade latency for consistency. The justification is that the "right" answer is per-use-case, not per-system.

Full answer

"CAP says a distributed system, during a network partition, can't have both consistency and availability — it must sacrifice one. The common 'pick 2 of 3' phrasing is misleading: partition tolerance isn't optional in a real distributed system (networks fail), so the real choice is C vs A when a partition occurs."

  • CP (consistency over availability): on a partition, refuse requests that can't be made consistent rather than serve possibly-wrong data. Banking, inventory, anything where correctness > uptime. Examples: traditional RDBMS with strong consistency, etcd/ZooKeeper, HBase.
  • AP (availability over consistency): keep serving, accept temporary inconsistency, converge later (eventual consistency). Social feeds, catalogs, shopping carts. Examples: Cassandra, DynamoDB (tunable), Riak.
  • Consistency spectrum: strong (every read sees the latest write) → eventual (reads may lag but converge), with middle grounds (read-your-writes, causal, bounded staleness). Many systems offer tunable consistency via quorums (R + W > N).
  • PACELC extends it: if Partition, choose A or C; Else (normal ops), choose Latency or Consistency. Strong consistency costs latency even when nothing's broken.

Q47

How do you design an idempotent REST API / data service?

apiidempotencyJD-relevant
How to think about this

The JD lists REST APIs / data services, and idempotency carries over from pipelines: clients retry, so the same request must not cause duplicate effects. The interviewer wants you to know which HTTP methods are naturally idempotent, how to make POST safe with an idempotency key, and the practical concerns of a data API — pagination, async for big jobs, versioning, rate limiting.

Decision justification

I make write endpoints idempotent with a client-supplied idempotency key: the server records the key + result, so a retried request returns the original result instead of acting twice. I justify this because networks make retries inevitable — a client that times out doesn't know if the write landed, and without a key it will double-charge/double-insert. I lean on HTTP semantics (GET/PUT/DELETE idempotent by design; POST isn't, hence the key). For data services specifically, I design pagination (cursor-based for stable large result sets), async + job-status for long/bulk operations (return 202 + a status URL rather than blocking), versioning (so I can evolve without breaking clients — mirrors Q7), and rate limiting to protect the backend.

Full answer
  • Use HTTP method semantics. GET (safe, idempotent), PUT/DELETE (idempotent — repeating yields same state), POST (not idempotent — creates each time). So the risk concentrates on POST.
  • Idempotency keys for POST. Client sends a unique Idempotency-Key header; server stores key→result. First call executes and records; retries with the same key return the stored result without re-executing. (This is how Stripe makes payments retry-safe.)
  • Pagination. Never return unbounded result sets. Prefer cursor/keyset pagination over offset for large, changing data — stable and efficient.
  • Async for heavy/bulk operations. A large export/transform shouldn't block an HTTP request — return 202 Accepted + a job id; client polls a status endpoint or gets a webhook. Decouples request latency from work.
  • Versioning. Version the API (URL or header) so schema/behavior can evolve without breaking existing clients — same backward-compatibility discipline as data schemas (Q7).
  • Protect & observe. Rate limiting/throttling, auth, input validation, consistent error codes, and metrics/logging on the service.

Idempotency-key handling (pseudocode)
def handle_post(req):
    key = req.headers["Idempotency-Key"]
    cached = store.get(key)
    if cached: return cached            # retry → return original result
    result = do_work(req.body)        # execute exactly once
    store.put(key, result, ttl=24h)   # record under the key
    return result
⚑ Remember this

Idempotency is the same idea across the stack: in pipelines it's partition-overwrite/MERGE (Q3); in APIs it's the idempotency key. Both answer the same question — "the caller retried; don't do it twice."

What not to say

Don't say "POST is idempotent" — it isn't; that's exactly why you need an idempotency key.

Don't design a synchronous endpoint that runs a 10-minute export inline — it'll time out and clients will retry, multiplying the load. Go async with a job status.

▸ Spark / Glue performance — the checklist

  • Measure first. Spark UI → slowest stage → classify (shuffle / skew / spill / small files / under-parallelized).
  • Read less: Parquet/ORC, predicate pushdown, column pruning, partition pruning. Biggest free win.
  • Kill shuffles: broadcast the small side; filter before wide ops; enable AQE; don't repartition() needlessly (coalesce() to shrink).
  • Fix skew: AQE skew-join → salt the hot key → isolate NULL/sentinel keys.
  • Fix spill: more executor memory or smaller partitions so working set fits.
  • Right-size compute last: DPUs / worker type only after the plan is efficient.

▸ Numbers every data engineer should know

  • Target file size: 128MB – 1GB (Parquet). Below ~128MB → small-files problem.
  • Spark default shuffle partitions: 200 (often wrong — tune or use AQE).
  • Broadcast join threshold: ~10MB default; can push to 100s of MB if executors have memory.
  • Snowflake micro-partition: ~50–500MB compressed, auto-managed.
  • Lambda max runtime: 15 min. Past that → Glue/EMR.
  • Partition sizing: aim for partitions in the 100s of MB; avoid high-cardinality partition keys.
  • Latency ladder: memory ns → SSD µs → network ms → cross-region 10s–100s ms.

▸ Airflow gotchas

  • Logical date = data period, not run time. The run "for 06-07" fires at 06-08 (interval end).
  • Tasks must be idempotent — they will be retried/re-run/backfilled.
  • Poke sensors starve workers — use deferrable=True or event-driven (Datasets).
  • Don't compute on the worker — orchestrate Glue/Spark/SQL; don't crunch data in a PythonOperator.
  • Template {{ ds }}, never datetime.now() — keeps runs reproducible.
  • Model data dependencies (Datasets), not cron timing — no "run at 4am and hope upstream's done."
  • Keep DAG-file top level light — the scheduler parses it constantly.

▸ Data-quality dimensions

  • Completeness — no missing rows/values; expected partitions present.
  • Validity — types, ranges, formats, allowed values.
  • Uniqueness — no unexpected duplicates; PK holds.
  • Consistency — cross-table/system agreement; referential integrity.
  • Timeliness — data fresh / on time vs SLA.
  • Accuracy — values reconcile to a trusted reference.
  • Enforce: on ingest (reject early) + before serving (gate). Critical = circuit-break; minor = warn; suspect rows = quarantine.

▸ Incident-response runbook

  • 1. Triage — severity + blast radius (lineage). Customer-facing?
  • 2. Contain — stop bad data publishing (circuit-break). A delay beats wrong data.
  • 3. Communicate — tell stakeholders early: impact + ETA + "don't trust X yet."
  • 4. Mitigate — fastest safe fix: retry / resume from failed task / failover.
  • 5. Verify — DQ + reconciliation before all-clear.
  • 6. RCA — blameless postmortem + a concrete prevention (check/alert/fix).

▸ Words that score points

  • Idempotent — re-running yields the same result (partition overwrite / MERGE / idempotency key).
  • Effectively-once — at-least-once delivery + idempotent writes (the honest "exactly-once").
  • Watermark — bound on lateness for event-time processing.
  • Predicate / projection pushdown — read less by filtering & column-pruning at the source.
  • Partition pruning — skip folders that can't match the filter.
  • Shuffle — the costly redistribution at wide-transformation / stage boundaries.
  • Micro-partition — Snowflake's automatic columnar storage unit.
  • Backfill / point-in-time — reprocess history deterministically; join to dimension as-of-then (SCD-2).
  • Data contract — enforced producer↔consumer agreement; shift-left breakage detection.
  • SLA / SLO / error budget — measurable reliability targets tied to consumer needs.
  • Lineage — dataset dependency graph → blast radius + root cause.
  • Expand/contract — backward-compatible schema migration under live traffic.
  • Strangler migration — parallel-run, validate parity, cut over incrementally.
